ADHD

  • Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by a consistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development.

Inattention:

  • This refers to difficulties in sustaining focus, poor organizational skills, and a tendency to make careless mistakes. Individuals with ADHD may have trouble staying on task, following detailed instructions, or completing tasks.

Hyperactivity

  • This manifests as excessive physical movement and fidgeting, an inability to stay still for appropriate periods, especially in calm or quiet settings. Hyperactive individuals often feel restless and may engage in activities with a high level of energy and at inappropriate times.

Impulsivity

  • Impulsive behaviors in ADHD include acting without thinking about the consequences, hasty actions that occur in the moment without first considering other responses, and a desire for immediate rewards or inability to delay gratification.

Objective

  • ADHD can affect social interactions and relationships. Children and adults with undiagnosed ADHD may have difficulties in social settings, potentially leading to misunderstandings and conflicts. Early diagnosis can lead to interventions that improve social skills and relationship management.
  • Identifying risk factors and intervening early in life can help to mitigate the severity of ADHD symptoms and improve long-term outcomes.

How to detect ADHD

  • We can take participants' personal characteristic data (site of data collection, age, gender, handedness, performance IQ, verbal IQ, and full scale IQ), without any fMRI data, as input to a supervised learning model to generate diagnostic prediction.

About the Data

  • Participant Demographics : The dataset includes a diverse range of participants, typically children and adolescents, with varying degrees of ADHD severity. This diversity allows for comprehensive research into the condition.
  • Neuroimaging Data: The primary focus of the collection was on neuroimaging data, specifically resting-state functional MRI (fMRI) scans. These scans are crucial for studying brain function, particularly in the context of ADHD, as they show brain activity patterns when a subject is not engaged in a specific task.
  • Structural MRI Data: In addition to functional MRI, the dataset also includes structural MRI data, providing detailed images of the brain's anatomy. This helps in understanding any structural differences associated with ADHD.
  • Phenotypic Data: Along with imaging data, the dataset comprises extensive phenotypic information. This includes clinical assessments, diagnostic interviews, behavioral data, and other relevant information about the participants, like age, gender, IQ, and medication status.
  • Open Access and Anonymization : The ADHD-200 dataset was made publicly available to the research community while ensuring the anonymity of the participants. This open access has facilitated widespread research and analysis by different groups, leading to numerous insights into ADHD.
